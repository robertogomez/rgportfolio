Semi-Autonomous Robotics Research

Position: Research Assistant
College: Dept. of Electrical and Computer Engineering, SDSU
Date: June 2012 - August 2012
Fields: Robotics, Electronics, C Programming

I have always believed that it's important to acquire practical, real-life experience outside of the classroom. To that end I was selected to participate in the 2012 NSF/MESA Summer Team Internship Program. As a research assistant for the SDSU Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, I developed a semi-autonomous, mobile robot with object avoidance capabilities. Based on the popular iRobot Create® platform, it used two ultrasonic sensors for object detection. For interfacing between the platform and sensors, I programmed an Arduino Mega microcontroller. It was responsible for interpreting the sensor data, determining if obstructions were in the robot's path, and issuing movement commands.

While it was a simple project as far as robotics go, it involved many disciplines, such as electrical, computer, and mechanical engineering. First, I determined how to connect all the components so that they would all function as a single system. For example, I created a simple circuit on a breadboard that delivered power from the iRobot® to the sensors and microcontroller. Then I programmed the Arduino (which essentially uses C/C++ language) to analyze the pulse width output signals from the two sensors. If an object was detected within ten inches, the microcontroller would send a sequence of bytes to the platform through serial communication to initiate a simple avoidance maneuver that consisted of stopping, rotating a number of degrees, and continuing forward. Lastly, I designed a few parts in SolidWorks to provide mounting surfaces for the components. The parts were formed from Plexiglas using a CNC machine in the SDSU engineering machine shop.

Looking back, this research has given me a great sense of accomplishment. It has definitely increased my knowledge and appreciation for robotics and embedded systems. For more information view the research poster my partner and I created (he worked on a website for the Retired Senior Volunteer Patrol). If you would like to know some of the technical details of the robot I can send you my report for the project.

Quality Assurance Internship

Position: Quality Assurance Intern I & II
Company: General Atomics Electronic Systems, Inc.
Date: May 2008 - August 2008, July 2009 - August 2009
Fields: Quality Assurance, Mechanical Engineering

As it so happens I was also involved in MESA's first Summer Team Internship event in 2008. I am proud to have been a part of this successful program, now in its fifth year, on two separate occasions. That year, when I was studying mechanical engineering at SDSU, I worked for General Atomics Electronic Systems, Inc. (GA-ESI) as a quality assurance (QA) intern. The QA work I did was for sophisticated aircraft launching and arresting systems called EMALS and AAG. What I primarily did was document the change control process of parts and materials that did not meet their specifications. Some of my responsibilities included gathering non-conformance reports from inspectors, writing supplier's disposition request (SDR) forms to record deficient items, and maintaining a spreadsheet to log and track said SDR's. As my first internship experience, I really enjoyed contributing to such an important and fast-paced project.

In the summer of 2009 I returned to work for GA-ESI again as a QA intern. I was given much more of a hands-on position as I assisted inspecting parts and materials to ensure they met the drawings and specifications required by engineers. In this position I learned first hand the importance of quality assurance. Although my work in these internships did not involve software development, I believe that they have given me the experience of working in a large, project-based company that is valuable in any organization.
